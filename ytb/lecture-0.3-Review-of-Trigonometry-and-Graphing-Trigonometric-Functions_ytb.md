● [0:30]. Initial Side and Terminal Side of an Angle 
     ◉ Initial Side: This is the starting position of the angle before any rotation occurs. It is typically aligned with the positive
                           x-axis in a standard position.
     ◉ Terminal Side: After rotating by the given angle measure (in degrees or radians), this is the final position where the side ends up.
                        
● [1:02]. Positive and Negative Angles 
     ◉ Counterclockwise: This direction follows the standard mathematical convention for positive angles.
     ◉ Clockwise: Rotations in this direction are considered negative in standard mathematical terms.

● [8:38]. Coterminal Angles 
     ◉ Coterminal angles are angles that share the same terminal side when drawn in standard position. They differ by 
         multiples of 360 degrees (or 2π or -2π radians). To find a coterminal angle within a specific range, adjust the given 
         angle by adding or subtracting these multiples.
     ◉ Examples:
          ○ Find two positive and two negative coterminal angles for 60 degrees:
               ■ Positive: 420°, 780°
               ■ Negative: -300°, -660°

● [16:17]. Relationship between the Sides of a Right Triangle and the Unit Circle 
     ◉ [17:15].  A right triangle has one 90-degree angle and two shorter sides called legs.
          ○ The side opposite the angle you're looking at is called the opposite side OPP.
          ○ The side next to the angle (but not the hypotenuse) is the adjacent side ADJ.
          ○ The longest side, opposite the right angle, is the hypotenuse HYP.
                                    
● [17:36]. Definition of Sine, Cosine, and Tangent 
     ◉ sin(θ) = OPP / HYP
     ◉ cos(θ) = ADJ / HYP
     ◉ tan(θ) = OPP / ADJ
                        
● [19:35]. Definition of Cosecant, Secant, and Cotangent 
     ◉ csc(θ) = HYP / OPP
     ◉ sec(θ) = HYP / ADJ
     ◉ cot(θ) = ADJ / OPP
                        
● [20:28]. Relationship between the Coordinates of a Point on the Unit Circle and the Trigonometric Functions of the Angle 
     ◉ sin(θ) = y / 1 = y ;  csc(θ) = 1 / y
     ◉ cos(θ) = x / 1 = x ;  sec(θ) = 1 / x
     ◉ tan(θ) = y / x ;      cot(θ) = x / y

● [27:35]. Reference Angles 
     ◉ Reference angles make it possible to evaluate trigonometric functions for angles outside the first quadrant.
         They can also be used to find (x,y) coordinates for those angles. We will use the reference angle of the angle of 
         rotation combined with the quadrant in which the terminal side of the angle lies. 
         The absolute values of the cosine and sine of an angle are the same as those of the reference angle. We will use the 
         reference angle of the angle of rotation combined with the quadrant in which the terminal side of the angle lies. 
     ◉ Measure the angle between the terminal side of the given angle and the horizontal axis. That is the reference angle (acute angle).
     ◉ [28:55]. Case 1: Angle in the First Quadrant: θ
     ◉ [29:20]. Case 2: Angle in the Second Quadrant: π - θ
     ◉ [30:40]. Case 3: Angle in the Third Quadrant:  θ - π
     ◉ [31:54]. Case 4: Angle in the Fourth Quadrant: 2π - θ
            
● [33:06]. Use of Reference Angles to Find Trigonometric Functions 
     ◉ [33:40]. 🧩 Example – : Find sin(5π/3), cos(5π/3), and tan(5π/3) Using Reference Angles
          ○ Step 1: Locate the Quadrant of the Angle: Fourth Quadrant
          ○ Step 2: Graph the Angle: Terminal Side and Initial Side
          ○ Step 3: Find the Reference Angle: 2π − (5π / 3) = π / 3
          ○ Step 4: Find the Trigonometric Functions of the Reference Angle
               ■ sin(π / 3)   = √3 / 2
               ■ cos(π / 3)   = 1 / 2
               ■ tan(π / 3)   = √3
          ○ Step 5: Use the Mnemonic "All Students Take Calculus" to Determine the Sign of the Trigonometric 
                           Functions of the Original Angle
               ■ sin(5π / 3)  = −√3 / 2
               ■ cos(5π / 3)  = 1 / 2
               ■ tan(5π / 3)  = −√3

● [1:05:00]. Translations of Trigonometric Functions 
     ◉ Graphs of Functions in the Form y = a ∙ sin(b∙x - c) or y = a ∙ cos(b∙x - c)
     ◉ Find the Horizontal Translation: Factor out b and observe "c/b"

     ◉ Horizontal Translation:
          ○ If it is -c/b, the translation is to the right
          ○ If it is +c/b, the translation is to the left
               ■ x + c/b = x - (-c/b)
