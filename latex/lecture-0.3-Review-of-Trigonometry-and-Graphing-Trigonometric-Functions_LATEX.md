● [0:30](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=SzLF-wLZF_I&list=PLF797E961509B4EB5&index=3&t=30). Initial Side and Terminal Side of an Angle [📷image](../img/Calculus 1 Lecture 0.3/[0-30]-01.png)
     ◉ Initial Side: This is the starting position of the angle before any rotation occurs. It is typically aligned with the positive x-axis in a standard position.
     ◉ Terminal Side: After rotating by the given angle measure (in degrees or radians), this is the final position where the side ends up.

                        
● [1:02](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=SzLF-wLZF_I&list=PLF797E961509B4EB5&index=3&t=62). Positive and Negative Angles [📷image](../img/Calculus 1 Lecture 0.3/[1-02]-01.png)
     ◉ Counterclockwise: This direction follows the standard mathematical convention for positive angles.
     ◉ Clockwise: Rotations in this direction are considered negative in standard mathematical terms.

● [8:38](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=SzLF-wLZF_I&list=PLF797E961509B4EB5&index=3&t=518). Coterminal Angles 
     ◉ Coterminal angles are angles that share the same terminal side when drawn in standard position. They differ by 
         multiples of $360^\circ$ (or $2\pi$ or $-2\pi$ radians). To find a coterminal angle within a specific range, adjust the given 
         angle by adding or subtracting these multiples.
     ◉ 🧩 Examples:
          ○ Find two positive and two negative coterminal angles for $60^\circ$:
               ■ Positive: $420^\circ$, $780^\circ$
               ■ Negative: $-300^\circ$, $-660^\circ$

● [16:17](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=SzLF-wLZF_I&list=PLF797E961509B4EB5&index=3&t=977). Relationship between the Sides of a Right Triangle and the Unit Circle [📷image](../img/Calculus 1 Lecture 0.3/[16-17]-01.png)
     ◉ [17:15](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=SzLF-wLZF_I&list=PLF797E961509B4EB5&index=3&t=1035).  A right triangle has one 90-degree angle and two shorter sides called legs.
          ○ The side opposite the angle you're looking at is called the opposite side (OPP).
          ○ The side next to the angle (but not the hypotenuse) is the adjacent side (ADJ).
          ○ The longest side, opposite the right angle, is the hypotenuse (HYP).

                                    
● [17:36](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=SzLF-wLZF_I&list=PLF797E961509B4EB5&index=3&t=1056). Definition of Sine, Cosine, and Tangent [📷image](../img/Calculus 1 Lecture 0.3/[16-17]-01.png)
$\rule{0pt}{}$
     ◉ $\sin(\theta)=\dfrac{\text{OPP}}{\text{HYP}}$
     $\rule{0pt}{}$
     ◉ $\cos(\theta)=\dfrac{\text{ADJ}}{\text{HYP}}$
     $\rule{0pt}{}$
     ◉ $\tan(\theta)=\dfrac{\text{OPP}}{\text{ADJ}}$

                        
● [19:35](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=SzLF-wLZF_I&list=PLF797E961509B4EB5&index=3&t=1175). Definition of Cosecant, Secant, and Cotangent [📷image](../img/Calculus 1 Lecture 0.3/[16-17]-01.png)
$\rule{0pt}{}$
     ◉ $\csc(\theta)=\dfrac{\text{HYP}}{\text{OPP}}$
     $\rule{0pt}{}$
     ◉ $\sec(\theta)=\dfrac{\text{HYP}}{\text{ADJ}}$
     $\rule{0pt}{}$
     ◉ $\cot(\theta)=\dfrac{\text{ADJ}}{\text{OPP}}$

                        
● [20:28](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=SzLF-wLZF_I&list=PLF797E961509B4EB5&index=3&t=1228). Relationship between the Coordinates of a Point on the Unit Circle and the Trigonometric Functions of the Angle [📷image](../img/Calculus 1 Lecture 0.3/[16-17]-01.png)
$\rule{0pt}{}$
     ◉ $\sin(\theta)=\dfrac{y}{1}=y$, $\csc(\theta)=\dfrac{1}{y}$
     $\rule{0pt}{}$
     ◉ $\cos(\theta)=\dfrac{x}{1}=x$, $\sec(\theta)=\dfrac{1}{x}$
     $\rule{0pt}{}$
     ◉ $\tan(\theta)=\dfrac{y}{x}$, $\cot(\theta)=\dfrac{x}{y}$

● [24:36](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=SzLF-wLZF_I&list=PLF797E961509B4EB5&index=3&t=1476). Review of Quadrants in the Cartesian Plane [📷image](../img/Calculus 1 Lecture 0.3/[24-36]-01.png)
     ◉ A̳S̳T̳C̳: The signs of the trigonometric functions for angles in each of the four quadrants can 
                     be remembered by means of the rule “A̳ll S̳tudents T̳ake C̳alculus”.
          ○ Quadrant I: (A̳) All trigonometric functions are positive.
          ○ Quadrant II: (S̳) $\sin(\theta)>0$
          ○ Quadrant III: (T̳) $\tan(\theta)>0$
          ○ Quadrant IV: (C̳) $\cos(\theta)>0$


● [27:35](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=SzLF-wLZF_I&list=PLF797E961509B4EB5&index=3&t=1655). Reference Angles [📷image](../img/Calculus 1 Lecture 0.3/[27-35]-01.png)
     ◉ Reference angles make it possible to evaluate trigonometric functions for angles outside the first quadrant.
         They can also be used to find $(x,y)$ coordinates for those angles. We will use the reference angle of the angle of 
         rotation combined with the quadrant in which the terminal side of the angle lies. 
     ◉ Measure the angle between the terminal side of the given angle and the horizontal axis. That is the reference angle (acute angle).
     ◉ [28:55](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=SzLF-wLZF_I&list=PLF797E961509B4EB5&index=3&t=1735). Case 1: Angle in the First Quadrant: $\theta$
     ◉ [29:20](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=SzLF-wLZF_I&list=PLF797E961509B4EB5&index=3&t=1760). Case 2: Angle in the Second Quadrant: $\pi-\theta$
     ◉ [30:40](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=SzLF-wLZF_I&list=PLF797E961509B4EB5&index=3&t=1840). Case 3: Angle in the Third Quadrant: $\theta-\pi$
     ◉ [31:54](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=SzLF-wLZF_I&list=PLF797E961509B4EB5&index=3&t=1914). Case 4: Angle in the Fourth Quadrant: $2\pi-\theta$

            
● [33:06](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=SzLF-wLZF_I&list=PLF797E961509B4EB5&index=3&t=1986). Use of Reference Angles to Find Trigonometric Functions [📷image](../img/Calculus 1 Lecture 0.3/[33-06]-01.png)
     ◉ [33:40](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=SzLF-wLZF_I&list=PLF797E961509B4EB5&index=3&t=2020). 🧩 Example – Find $\sin(5\pi/3)$, $\cos(5\pi/3)$, and $\tan(5\pi/3)$ Using Reference Angles
          ○ Step 1: Locate the Quadrant of the Angle: Fourth Quadrant
          ○ Step 2: Graph the Angle: Terminal Side and Initial Side
          $\rule{0pt}{}$
          ○ Step 3: Find the Reference Angle: $2\pi - \dfrac{5\pi}{3} = \dfrac{\pi}{3}$
          $\rule{0pt}{}$
          ○ Step 4: Find the Trigonometric Functions of the Reference Angle
          $\rule{0pt}{}$
               ■ $\sin\!\left(\dfrac{\pi}{3}\right)=\dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$
               $\rule{0pt}{}$
               ■ $\cos\!\left(\dfrac{\pi}{3}\right)=\dfrac{1}{2}$
               $\rule{0pt}{}$
               ■ $\tan\!\left(\dfrac{\pi}{3}\right)=\sqrt{3}$
               $\rule{0pt}{}$
          ○ Step 5: Use the Mnemonic "All Students Take Calculus" to Determine the Sign of the Trigonometric 
                           Functions of the Original Angle
                           $\rule{0pt}{}$
               ■ $\sin\!\left(\dfrac{5\pi}{3}\right)=-\dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$
               $\rule{0pt}{}$
               ■ $\cos\!\left(\dfrac{5\pi}{3}\right)=\dfrac{1}{2}$
               $\rule{0pt}{}$
               ■ $\tan\!\left(\dfrac{5\pi}{3}\right)=-\sqrt{3}$

● [1:05:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=SzLF-wLZF_I&list=PLF797E961509B4EB5&index=3&t=3900). Translations of Trigonometric Functions [📷image](../img/Calculus 1 Lecture 0.3/[1-05-00]-01.png)
     ◉ Graphs of Functions in the Form $y=a\cdot\sin(bx-c)$ or $y=a\cdot\cos(bx-c)$
     $\rule{0pt}{}$
     ◉ Find the Horizontal Translation: Factor out $b$ and observe $\dfrac{c}{b}$
     $\rule{0pt}{}$
          ○ $y=a\cdot\sin\!\big[b(x-\dfrac{c}{b})\big]$ or $y=a\cdot\cos\!\big[b(x-\dfrac{c}{b})\big]$
          $\rule{0pt}{}$
     ◉ Horizontal Translation:
     $\rule{0pt}{}$
          ○ If it is $-\dfrac{c}{b}$, the translation is to the right
          $\rule{0pt}{}$
          ○ If it is $+\dfrac{c}{b}$, the translation is to the left
          $\rule{0pt}{}$
               ■ $x+\dfrac{c}{b}=x-(-\dfrac{c}{b})$
               $\rule{0pt}{}$
     ◉ [1:10:13](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=SzLF-wLZF_I&list=PLF797E961509B4EB5&index=3&t=4213). 🧩 Example – $y=3\cdot\cos(2x+\dfrac{\pi}{2})$
          ○ Amplitude: $3$
          ○ Period: $\pi$
          ○ Horizontal Translation: $\dfrac{\pi}{4}$ to the left [📷image](../img/Calculus 1 Lecture 0.3/[1-10-13]-01.png)
          $\rule{0pt}{}$
               ■ $y=3\cdot\cos\!\Big[2\big(x+\dfrac{\pi}{4}\big)\Big]$
